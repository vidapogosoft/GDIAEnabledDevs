The question is not "can AI replace what we do?" — it is "what do skilled people contribute that AI cannot substitute?"

The answer is the same in every discipline where AI has become capable: judgment — knowing which problem is worth solving, shaping how the work gets framed, and deciding whether the output meets the intent. AI executes with remarkable precision. It does not decide what is worth executing. That distinction is what makes the Triad's contribution more important in agentic development, not less. The role of every person in this model shifts from production to direction.

Before you begin — the right mental model

Think of Claude Code as an exceptionally skilled contractor who works through the night. They are fast, thorough, and technically capable — far more so than any single developer. But they are not in your office. They were not on your stakeholder call. They did not hear you say "you know, handle it the way we did for the Motor claims project." When they hit an ambiguity in your spec, they do not stop and ask — they make a reasonable call and keep building.

What you find on your desk in the morning is a direct reflection of three things: what you specified, the context you provided, and your review before it ships. Claude Code cannot read what you were thinking when you wrote "handle the usual edge cases." It has no access to the conversation behind the requirement. Context it was not given does not exist. Intent it was not told cannot be inferred.

Spec input is everything
Vague input produces plausible output — not correct output. Every ambiguity in the spec becomes a decision Claude makes on your behalf.
Context cannot be assumed
Your system, your data model, your business rules, your constraints — none of it exists for Claude unless you explicitly include it in the spec.
Human review is non-negotiable
Review is not a formality performed on something that's probably fine. It is the mechanism that closes the gap between what you wrote and what you meant.

The Human in the Loop is not an optional checkpoint — it is the whole model. Claude gets you to the right building fast. You are still needed to check every room.

The shift: In traditional agile, an epic was a planning container — a bucket for user stories broken down in refinement. In agentic AI development, an epic IS the specification. It is the direct input to Claude Code. There is no human to fill the gaps.

The Spec-Driven SDLC Flow

  • 1PO frames the problem — translates customer and business pain into a problem worth solving. Defines measurable outcomes, personas, and constraints. Uses Claude to surface insights from discovery recordings. This is where human judgment about what matters is irreplaceable.
  • 2BA shapes the functional specification — takes validated requirements and translates them into a precision implementation brief. Uses Claude to model scenarios and draft rules; curates, approves, and owns every output. The BA does not just document — they exercise functional judgment at every step.
  • 3Tech Lead architects the AI's understanding — stops writing boilerplate, starts building the precise context that directs Claude Code: brownfield constraints, ContextForge standards, reference patterns, tech debt boundaries, and architectural decisions Claude cannot infer.
  • 4Triad reviews spec together — PO confirms business accuracy, BA confirms completeness, Tech Lead confirms technical feasibility and architectural alignment.
  • 5Spec passes Readiness Gate — all mandatory sections complete, no ambiguous language, CDM verified, tech solution signed off by Tech Lead. The gate exists because the trust placed in generated code must be earned before it runs.
  • 6Claude Code executes — humans judge the output — generates production code, tests, and documentation. Engineer reviews every output against acceptance scenarios. PO accepts against business intent. The final check is always human.
⚠️

The critical insight: The word "stories" now describes something that no longer needs to exist as a separate artefact. Acceptance scenarios (GWT) live inside the spec. Decomposition happens in the spec's requirement sections. The spec IS the requirement AND the implementation brief.

The Triad: PO · BA · Tech Lead

A spec cannot be produced by one role alone. The PO brings business intent, the BA brings functional precision, and the Tech Lead brings technical grounding. All three co-own the spec.

��

Common failure mode: BA writes the spec, hands it to the Tech Lead who adds a tech section at the end. This produces a spec where business rules are written without understanding technical constraints. The Triad must work together — not sequentially.

PO Product Owner

Owns: Why we build this. Business problem, value, KPIs, regulatory drivers, persona needs, priority.

Signs off: Problem statement, success metrics, persona definitions, out-of-scope decisions.

BA Business Analyst

Owns: What it must do. Business rules, acceptance scenarios, data operations, UX brief, edge cases, regulatory detail.

Signs off: All functional requirements sections. GWT completeness. CDM alignment.

TL Tech Lead

Owns: How it will be built. Architecture pattern, API design, event contracts, data schema, security, NFRs, Claude Code context files.

Signs off: All technical spec sections. CDM field verification. Holds the Readiness Gate.

XD optionalExperience Design Team

When engaged: Priority external-facing applications only. Not part of the squad. The PO initiates engagement via the design team lead — it is not the default for every epic.

Contributes to: UX strategy, interaction design, accessibility review, and design system compliance for customer-facing screens. XD input is appended to the UX & Prototype Brief (§10) before Tech Lead sign-off.

Timing: Engage at prototype brief stage (Phase 1) — not after screens are built. XD rework post-development is significantly more expensive than XD input pre-discovery.

��

Smaller teams — PO as BA: In smaller squads, the PO may also fulfil the BA authoring role. This is entirely workable. What matters is that every spec section is authored to the required standard and reviewed by someone other than the author. The role labels in this guide describe the function being performed, not a mandatory headcount. If the PO has the functional analysis skills, they can own the BA sections — the Tech Lead sign-off and the Readiness Gate criteria remain unchanged regardless of team size.

From Discovery to Spec: Using Voice, Webex & Prototypes

The richest source of business rules is not a document — it is a conversation. AI-assisted transcription and prototype-driven discussion turn raw discovery into structured spec input.

��️

Key principle: Record every requirements meeting. A 60-minute Webex session contains more business rules, edge cases, and exceptions than most BAs can capture manually in real time. Claude can extract, structure, and number those rules from the transcript in minutes.

Why Prototypes Are Critical

Anchors the conversation

A prototype gives stakeholders something concrete to react to. Discussions move from "what should it do" to "what happens when I do this" — which surfaces edge cases that abstract rule discussions miss entirely.

Drives the UX brief

Every screen in the prototype maps to a component, a state, and an entry/exit point in the spec's UX brief. The BA annotates the prototype walkthrough directly into the spec. Claude Code uses this to generate consistent frontend components.

Generates acceptance scenarios

Each state transition in the prototype is a GWT scenario. "When the user clicks Submit with an invalid amount" — the prototype shows this state; the BA writes the GWT. Prototype-driven GWT is more complete than rule-driven GWT alone.

Validates with stakeholders

A prototype review session with the business is 10x more productive than a written spec review. Stakeholders see the flow, raise corrections, and the BA captures them in real time — directly into the spec draft.

Module 05 — Epic as Specification

What an Epic IS Now

An epic in agentic AI development is a self-contained functional and technical specification. It is the complete set of information Claude Code needs to generate production-ready code — with no follow-up questions.

��

Definition: An Epic Specification is a versioned document that describes a single, coherent system capability at the level of detail required for autonomous AI code generation. It is complete when Claude Code can implement it correctly without any human clarification.

The Decomposition Model

Previously (Agile)

Theme → Epic → Feature → User Story → Sub-task

Each layer existed to decompose planning and estimation. The story was the delivery unit. Tech tasks were derived informally in standups.

Now (Agentic AI)

Programme → Epic Specification → Requirement → Acceptance Scenario

The Epic Spec is the delivery unit. Requirements within it define scope boundaries. Acceptance scenarios drive test generation. Claude Code handles decomposition.

The No-Questions Test

��

Before declaring a spec ready, each Triad member asks: "If I gave this document to Claude Code right now, what would it need to ask me before it could write the code?" If any answer exists — the spec is not ready. The Tech Lead runs this test formally at the Readiness Gate.

03Business Process & Operations ImpactAs-Is process, screen flow, customer & ops change — BA authors, PO confirms
BAPO
▶

This is the most commonly skipped section — and the one that produces the most expensive post-go-live failures. AI-generated code can implement the correct technical logic while simultaneously replacing human processes that operations teams have not been trained on, creating screens that no one manages, or automating steps that regulatory compliance requires to be manual. This section prevents all of that.

This section documents what exists today, what changes for customers and operations, and what the new journey looks like end to end. It is the bridge between the Business Problem Statement and the functional requirements — and it is what ensures AI-generated code is solving the right problem in the right operational context.

As-Is Process. Document the current process step by step. Tag each step with the role performing it, the system they use, and the time taken. Include: the trigger event, each manual step, the tools involved, the pain points, and the end state. This is not a value stream map — it is a clear factual narrative of exactly how the work is done today, written so that a developer who has never seen your operation understands it.

High-Level Screen Flow (To-Be). A numbered walkthrough of the new journey — screen by screen, step by step, actor by actor. Name every screen, every actor (human or system), and the outcome of each step. This becomes the direct source for the UX Brief (§10) and Acceptance Scenarios (§06). If a step is automated with no screen, say so explicitly: the AI needs to know this is a system action, not a missing screen.

Customer Impact. Explicitly state what changes for customers — their experience, communications, SLAs, and touchpoints. If no customer-facing change exists in this spec, state that explicitly and reference the future spec that will address it. Do not leave this blank — a blank field signals the team has not thought about it.

Operations Impact & Change Register. Document every operational process that is retired, introduced, or modified. Name the owner, the timing, and what retraining or tooling is required. This is the handoff document to the business change and training teams — it is a required output of the spec, not an afterthought.

06Functional Requirements & Acceptance ScenariosGWT — BA authors, TL verifies field names
BATL
▶

Acceptance scenarios are the single most important input for AI-assisted development. Each GWT scenario is an executable contract: a given state of the system, when a specific event occurs, produces a specific verifiable outcome. Claude Code uses these scenarios to generate code and tests simultaneously. Without them, AI-generated code cannot be meaningfully tested against intent.

Grouped by functional requirement. Each requirement has 2–5 GWT scenarios. Prototype walkthroughs are the primary source for GWT scenarios — each screen state in the prototype maps to at least one scenario. The Tech Lead verifies all entity field names and enum values match the CDM.

07Data SpecificationCDM entities and operations — TL authors
TL
▶

The Data Specification tells Claude Code which entities to read, which to write, and which to leave untouched. 1 — Specific, not generic

✗ "a claim with a high reserve value"
✓ "reserve=£150,000 (BR-002 threshold is £100,000)"
2 — Atomic trigger

When contains one action. "And" in When is a red flag — it means you have two scenarios hiding in one, or you are confusing setup with trigger.

✗ "When the user clicks Assign and confirms the modal"
✓ Split into two scenarios: Assign clicked · Confirm clicked
3 — Exhaustive Then

Then lists every observable outcome: field changes, events published, audit records, UI updates, notifications, error states. If a consequence is not in Then, it will not be tested.

✗ "Then the claim is assigned successfully"
✓ Status · handler_id · AuditEvent · published event · notification — all stated
4 — Independently runnable

Each scenario sets up its own state. No scenario depends on a previous one having run. Given must be a complete, self-contained setup — not "continuing from the previous scenario".

✗ Scenarios that form a chain where test 3 requires test 2 to have run first
✓ Each Given creates the exact system state needed from scratch
5 — CDM-aligned field names throughout

Every entity name, field name, and enum value in the scenario must use the CDM-verified name for the relevant line of business. A GWT scenario using a business alias instead of the CDM field name will cause Claude Code to generate code referencing a field that does not exist in the schema.

✗ "Then the reserve amount is updated to £35,000" — "reserve amount" is a business term

The Canonical Data Model

The CDM is Chubb's shared business language — a standardised definition of every key entity and field name across lines of business. Every spec references it. No AI-generated code may invent schema outside of it.

Why the CDM exists — the Chubb context

Insurance is a data business. Across every line of business — commercial, specialty, personal, marine — Chubb deals in the same fundamental concepts: policies, claims, insureds, handlers, reserves, events. The Canonical Data Model exists to give those concepts a single, shared definition. When a Claim is a Claim everywhere — same entity name, same field names, same enumerated values — systems can communicate without translation layers.

This is not a technical preference. It is how Chubb communicates as a company. A claims handler in Singapore, a coordinator in Philadelphia, and an analytics dashboard in London are all working with the same underlying data structure. That shared structure is what makes enterprise-scale intelligence possible.

Live North America Commercial
Live EMEA Commercial
Expanding Additional key lines of business

NA Commercial and EMEA each have their own CDM variation — shared core entities with line-of-business-specific extensions. As the CDM expands to further lines of business, the same pattern applies: shared foundation, scoped extensions.

Downstream benefits
✓ Global Data Platform — every system built to CDM emits data the GDP can consume directly, without ETL transformation to align field names
✓ Cross-LOB analytics — dashboards and regulatory reports built against CDM entities work across all systems that reference the same model
✓ Integration simplicity — services built by different squads at different times can communicate because they share the same entity contracts
✓ AI consistency — Claude Code generates code using CDM field names exactly, meaning every AI-built service produces CDM-compliant data by design from day one
⚠️

The failure mode this prevents: When a BA writes a GWT scenario referencing Claim.reserve_amount and the CDM field is actually Claim.estimated_reserve, Claude Code generates code referencing a non-existent field — and every downstream system that consumes that event receives non-standard data. One field name invented outside the CDM in one spec can create an integration problem that persists across multiple systems for years. The Tech Lead's CDM review is not a formality — it is what keeps the shared data model intact.

How to Create the UX Brief

��

Starting point: the prototype walkthrough recording. The HTML prototype is generated and reviewed with stakeholders during discovery (Module 04). Every screen state the stakeholder clicked through becomes a named component, a state machine entry, and at least one GWT scenario in §06. If a state is not in the recording, it will not be in the brief, and Claude Code will not build it.

  • Step 1 List every screen in the To-Be flow — pull directly from §03 (Business Process & Operations Impact). If a screen is in the To-Be flow but not in the UX brief, it will not be built. If a screen is in the UX brief but not in the To-Be flow, it is scope creep.

Roadmaps & Delivery Planning

Story points measured human cognitive effort to build. In agentic AI development, build effort is near-zero for code generation. The bottleneck shifts entirely — and so does how you plan.

��

The fundamental shift: In traditional agile, the question was "how long will it take to build?" In agentic AI development, the question becomes "when will the spec be ready, and when are the dependencies unblocked?" Build effort is no longer the critical path. Spec quality and dependency readiness are.

The Spec Cycle — One Unit of Delivery

  • 1Spec authoring — Triad writes §01–§11. Estimated in human days, not story points. Typical range: 2–8 days depending on domain complexity and number of business rules.
  • 2Readiness gate — Triad review and sign-off. Plan 1 day. If the spec fails the gate, estimate one more iteration loop (0.5–1 day) before re-review.
  • 3Claude Code execution — generates code, tests, and documentation. Hours to a day. Not the bottleneck. The engineer runs it; the spec does the work.
  • 4Review & acceptance — engineer reviews generated code against acceptance scenarios; PO signs off. Plan 1–3 days. Quality of this step is directly proportional to how well the GWT scenarios were written.
  • 5Deployment & human-only work — infrastructure, ops retraining, comms, regulatory submissions. Variable. Does not compress with AI — plan these as fixed lead times on the critical path.
Typical spec cycle duration
A well-scoped capability completes one full cycle in 1–3 weeks of calendar time — regardless of code complexity. The constraint is human availability for spec authoring, dependency readiness, and review — not build time.

Sizing a Delivery — When to Decompose

The discipline of decomposition still applies in the agentic world — but for different reasons. You decompose not because large epics are hard to build, but because large specs are hard to write well and impossible to review with confidence.

A well-sized spec

  • 10–15 acceptance scenarios
  • 8–12 business rules
  • 2–4 distinct screens
  • 1–2 CDM entity changes
  • One clear capability delivered end-to-end
  • Reviewable by the Triad in a single session

A spec that needs decomposing

  • 20+ acceptance scenarios across unrelated flows
  • Multiple independent screen journeys
  • Cross-team API dependencies with different readiness dates
  • Business rules owned by different stakeholders
  • Regulatory and non-regulatory components mixed together
��

How to decompose: Use §11 (Out of Scope) to name follow-on specs explicitly. Each spec delivers a working increment. The sequencing of those specs is your roadmap. You are not breaking stories out of an epic — you are sequencing dependent specifications, each one fully deliverable on its own.

⚠️

Dependencies are the new blocker. In traditional agile, blockers were often "we haven't designed this yet" — which AI eliminates. What remains are the real dependencies: data contracts, cross-team APIs, environment provisioning, regulatory approvals. A roadmap in the agentic world must be ruthlessly dependency-focused. If the CDM change request is not approved, the spec cannot be validated and the delivery cannot start — regardless of how fast Claude Code can build.

Delivery Sizing in Practice

When planning a quarter, ask these questions for each capability:

  1. Can the Triad write a complete spec for this in one authoring cycle? If not, decompose it into dependent specs now — not in refinement.
  2. What are the dependencies — and when are they ready? Map every CDM change, cross-team API, and regulatory approval. These set your earliest start date.
  3. What human-only work runs in parallel? Ops training, change comms, and deployment windows must be planned concurrently, not sequenced after code review.
  4. What is the review load? Complex domain areas or regulatory constraints require more triad review iterations. Factor this into the spec authoring estimate.
  5. Is this the right quarter? If a critical dependency won't be ready until mid-quarter, the spec cycle can't start until then. Plan accordingly — don't plan to start on day one of the quarter if a dependency lands on day 30.

Claude is a collaborative authoring tool for the Triad — used throughout discovery, spec drafting, and review. Always provide full context; Claude has no memory of your project between sessions.

General principles — applies to every Claude prompting session

Prompts, Agents, Skills & Workflows

Understanding the layered toolbox of AI-assisted development. A great prompt is the foundation — agents, skills, and workflows multiply that foundation into repeatable, scalable capability. How Prompts, Agents, Skills & Workflows Fit Together

��

The layered model: Every agentic AI capability ultimately rests on a prompt. A skill packages a proven prompt pattern for reuse. Each layer adds scope, autonomy, and complexity — start with a great prompt before building anything above it.

�� Prompt

Writing Good Prompts — The Thought Process

Most prompts fail not because the AI is incapable, but because the instruction is ambiguous, incomplete, or missing the context the AI needs to do the job correctly. Writing a good prompt is a design activity — it requires the same precision you apply to writing a business rule or acceptance scenario.

The 6-Step Prompting Framework

Step 1 — Define the goal precisely

Before writing a single word, ask: What is the exact output I need? Not "I want Claude to help with the spec" — but "I need a numbered list of business rules extracted from this transcript, each with a condition, constraint, and exception." The sharper the goal, the sharper the output.

Checklist: Can you describe the output in one sentence? If not, split into multiple prompts.

Step 2 — Establish role and context

Tell Claude who you are, what system you're working in, and what stage you're at. Claude calibrates its response to your stated role and context — a BA writing business rules needs different output than a TL writing API contracts for the same feature. Always include the spec ID and state that Claude Code will implement this spec directly; it changes how Claude weights precision and completeness.

Checklist: Have you stated your role? The spec ID? The platform? What Claude Code will do with this output?

Step 3 — Specify the output format

Claude follows structure instructions precisely. If you need numbered BRs in a specific format, show the format. If you need GWT blocks, give the template. Explicitly specifying the format produces consistently structured output that slots directly into your spec template — no manual reformatting needed.

Checklist: Have you shown the exact format with field labels? Specified whether you want markdown, numbered lists, or a specific template structure?

Step 4 — Set guardrails and constraints

Tell Claude what it should NOT do, assume, or invent. This is especially critical with system-specific names (CDM fields, API contracts, enum values) — Claude will use plausible-sounding alternatives if you don't constrain it. State constraints explicitly: "Only use the CDM field names I have provided. Do not invent new ones."

Checklist: What would Claude plausibly get wrong or over-generate? Have you explicitly excluded it?

Step 5 — Provide an example for precision tasks

For tasks where format and phrasing precision matter — business rule authoring, GWT generation, spec review — include one worked example in the prompt. "Format each rule exactly like this: [example]" produces far more consistent output than a description of the format alone. One good example is worth three paragraphs of instruction.

Checklist: Would a new team member understand the expected output from your description alone, or do they need to see an example?

Step 6 — Plan your review step before you submit

Never accept the first output as final. Before you submit the prompt, decide what review prompt you will run after it. The standard review is: "Apply the no-questions test to what you just wrote. What would a developer need to ask before implementing it?" Build the review into your prompting process — not as an afterthought.

Checklist: Have you planned a follow-up review prompt? Have you identified which role (BA, TL, PO) should validate the output?

Building a Good Agent

An agent is not a better prompt — it is a fundamentally different mode of operation. Where a prompt produces a single response and stops, an agent is given a goal and a set of tools, and works autonomously across multiple steps until the goal is reached. Understanding that difference is the foundation of building agents that are useful rather than unpredictable.

Prompt vs Agent — The Critical Difference

�� Prompt (single-turn)

You provide input. Claude produces output. Done. You decide the next step, craft the next prompt, and drive the conversation forward. Ideal when the task is well-defined, the inputs are ready, and human judgement is needed after each step.

Control: Full. Every step is human-driven.

�� Agent (multi-step)

You provide a goal and tools. The agent plans, executes, observes the result, and adjusts — repeating until it reaches the goal or hits a defined boundary. Ideal when the task has multiple steps, conditional branches, or requires information-gathering before synthesis.

Control: Delegated. The agent drives steps; you define the boundary.

��

The rule of thumb: If you find yourself running the same prompt 4–5 times in sequence — each feeding the output of the last — and the steps are deterministic (no human judgement required mid-way), that sequence is a candidate to become an agent. If human review is needed at each step, keep it as sequential prompts.

The Anatomy of a Well-Defined Agent

Every agent needs five things defined before it runs. Missing any one of them produces an agent that either does too little, too much, or the wrong thing entirely.

1 — Goal (what done looks like)

The goal is not a task description — it is an exit condition. "Review the spec" is a task. "Produce a numbered list of every section where ambiguous language appears, with the exact phrase quoted and a suggested replacement, and stop when all sections have been checked" is a goal. The agent needs to know when it is finished.

Test: Can you tell by inspecting the output whether the agent succeeded? If not, rewrite the goal as a measurable exit condition.

2 — Tools (what it can touch)

Tools are the actions the agent can take: reading files, searching code, running commands, calling APIs. Specify exactly which tools are needed and — critically — which are off-limits. An agent given write access when it only needs read access is an unnecessary risk. Match tool access to the minimum required for the goal.

Common tools for Triad agents: file reading (specs, transcripts, CDM), grep/search (pattern matching across docs), structured output writing (findings reports). Rarely needed: code execution, external API calls.

3 — Scope (what it can look at)

Scope defines the boundaries of the agent's operating environment. Which files, directories, or data sources is it allowed to access? A spec validation agent should read the spec and the CDM — not crawl the entire codebase. Narrow scope produces faster, more focused agents and eliminates the risk of the agent acting on unintended inputs.

Always specify scope as paths or data sources, not as descriptions ("the relevant files"). Descriptions are ambiguous; paths are not.

4 — Reasoning loop (how it works toward the goal)

A good agent follows a Plan → Execute → Observe → Adjust loop. In the prompt, structure this explicitly: tell the agent to first plan its approach before taking any action, then execute one step at a time, then check whether the step output moves it toward the goal. Agents that skip the planning phase tend to take irreversible actions before they have enough information.

Include in the prompt: "Before taking any action, state your plan. After each step, state what you found and what you will do next."

5 — Boundaries (when to stop and escalate)

Agents should not guess when they hit ambiguity — they should stop and report. Define the escalation condition explicitly: "If you encounter a section where you cannot determine whether a rule is ambiguous without domain knowledge, flag it as 'needs human review' and continue rather than making a judgement." An agent that flags uncertainty is far more valuable than one that silently resolves it incorrectly.

Never-do list: never modify source files, never invent field names or enum values, never make assumptions about business intent — flag and continue.

Building a Good Skill

A skill is a packaged prompt pattern — a reusable, named capability that your team can invoke consistently without having to reconstruct the prompt from scratch each time. In Claude Code, skills are slash commands (/skill-name) that expand into a full, structured prompt. A well-built skill library turns individual prompting expertise into team-wide repeatable capability.

��

The packaging signal: The moment you find yourself copying a prompt from a previous session, or explaining to a colleague "here's the prompt I use for X", you have identified a skill candidate. If a prompt is valuable enough to share, it is valuable enough to package, version, and govern.

When to Package a Prompt into a Skill

Package it when...

  • You or your team run the same prompt pattern 3 or more times
  • The prompt requires precise formatting that is easy to get wrong
  • The output feeds a downstream process that needs consistency
  • Multiple roles need the same capability (e.g., all BAs run rule extraction)
  • The prompt contains boilerplate context (platform details, CDM field lists) that should not be re-typed each time

Keep it as a prompt when...

  • The task is genuinely one-off — you will never need it again
  • The inputs change so dramatically each time that a fixed template adds no value
  • You are still experimenting with the prompt — wait until it is proven before packaging
  • The skill would need to be re-explained each time it is used — a sign the trigger is unclear

Skill Anatomy — The Five Components

1 — Name & trigger

The skill name is its slash command: /extract-rules, /generate-gwt, /readiness-check. Names should be verb-noun, action-first, and self-explanatory to someone who has never used the skill before. The trigger is how the user invokes it — either directly (/extract-rules) or with a required argument (/extract-rules [paste transcript]).

Naming rule: if you need to explain the name, rename it. The name should make the output obvious.

2 — Description

One sentence: what the skill does and when to use it. This is what appears in the skill library and what a new team member reads before invoking it. Write it from the user's perspective: "Extracts business rules from a stakeholder transcript and formats them as BR-XXX entries ready for the spec template." Not a technical description of how it works internally.

3 — Required inputs

What must the user provide for the skill to work? Make inputs explicit and name them. "Paste the raw transcript below the command" is better than leaving users to guess. If the skill requires CDM field names, list them as a required input — do not allow the skill to run without them, because the output will be unreliable. Skills with unclear input requirements get used wrong and produce bad output.

If a required input is frequently unavailable (e.g., CDM not yet confirmed), add a flag: "If CDM not yet confirmed, add a flag to all generated field names: [UNVERIFIED — TL to confirm]"

4 — Output format

Define the exact structure the skill produces. Include the template with field labels in the skill definition. Consistent output format is the primary reason to package a prompt into a skill — if the output format varies, the downstream process that depends on it breaks. Lock the format and version it when it changes.

5 — Owner & governance

Every skill has an owner — the role responsible for keeping it accurate, versioning it when the underlying process changes, and deprecating it when it is no longer needed. For BA skills, the owner is the BA lead. For TL skills, the TL. A skill without an owner drifts — it produces subtly wrong output as processes change, and nobody notices until a spec defect appears.

Building a Good Workflow

A workflow is a defined end-to-end process — a sequence of prompts, skills, and agent runs, connected by structured handoffs, with human checkpoints inserted at the points where domain judgement is irreplaceable. A well-designed workflow makes spec authoring repeatable, auditable, and scalable across the Triad without depending on any individual's memory of "how we do it."

Workflow Design Principles

Each stage has a defined input, process, and output

A stage that cannot be described as "takes X, does Y, produces Z" is not a stage — it is an undefined gap. Every stage in a workflow must have a clear entry condition (what must be true before it starts), a defined process (which tool, skill, or agent runs), and a structured output that the next stage can consume without ambiguity.

Human checkpoints go where judgement is irreplaceable

Not every stage needs a human checkpoint — only the ones where domain knowledge, stakeholder relationships, or policy interpretation cannot be automated. Overloading a workflow with human gates defeats the purpose. The right question is: "Could an error here propagate silently through the rest of the workflow?" If yes, gate it. If no, let it flow.

Design for partial failure

Workflows break mid-run — a stakeholder is unavailable, a transcript is incomplete, a CDM field is not yet confirmed. Design each stage so its output is preserved even if the next stage cannot start. A workflow where stage 3 failure discards stages 1 and 2 output is a workflow that will be abandoned. Output at every stage should be a persistent artefact.

Handoff format is part of the workflow definition

The output of one stage is the input of the next. If the format is not locked down, the handoff breaks down. Specify the exact output structure for each stage — not "a list of business rules" but "business rules in BR-XXX format with condition, constraint, exception, TL flag fields". The handoff format is as important as the stage process itself.

Stakeholders who understand the session structure participate more efficiently. Unstructured sessions drift into scope expansion and leave exception paths unaddressed.

Step 3.2 — Running the session

Prototype walkthrough (45 min)

Walk screen by screen. For each screen, ask — do not describe — "What would happen if [state]?" Let stakeholders react to what they see. Capture every reaction, especially corrections ("no, that's not right — it should..."). Every correction is a business rule.

For every rule mentioned, ask two questions before moving on:

  • "Is this a hard block or a soft warning?" Hard block = validation rule. Soft warning = notification rule. The answer has direct implementation consequences.
  • "What happens when the rule cannot be satisfied?" This surfaces the exception path — the most commonly missed requirement in specs authored without this question.

Rule confirmation (20 min)

Present each rule hypothesis one at a time. For each:

  • "Is this rule correct as stated?" — if not, ask for the correction verbatim
  • "Is there a threshold or value we haven't captured?" — surfaces £amounts, percentages, time limits
  • "Are there exceptions to this rule?" — ask explicitly; stakeholders rarely volunteer exceptions unprompted
  • "Does this rule apply to all personas or only specific roles?" — catches permission-level constraints

If a stakeholder cannot confirm a rule ("I'd need to check the policy"), mark it as OPEN-Q and move on. Open questions are resolved post-session with the SME — they do not block the session.

⚠️

End every session with this question: "What would make this system wrong in a way that embarrassed us in production — something we haven't discussed today?" This single question surfaces more exception paths than any other technique. Give stakeholders 60 seconds of silence to think. The answer is always worth capturing.

Step 4.3 — Write fully specified GWT acceptance scenarios

Each GWT scenario is a contract. It must be precise enough to generate an automated test — which means every field name, every enum value, and every side-effect must be explicitly stated using CDM-verified names. A GWT with vague language generates a test that passes but does not verify the right behaviour.

Weak GWT — what Claude produces from this

Given: A valid FNOL exists and a handler is available When: The system assigns the claim Then: The claim is assigned and the handler is notified

Claude generates a test that checks a boolean "assigned" field, ignores the AuditEvent, ignores the published event, uses invented field names, and passes even when the assignment logic is wrong. This GWT is useless.

Strong GWT — full specification

Given: FNOL CLM-9981 has peril_category=MOTOR_THEFT, reserve_estimate=£35,000. Handler Alice: active_claim_count=32, skill_tags=[MOTOR_THEFT], seniority=STANDARD, last_assignment_date=2024-03-10. Handler Bob: active_claim_count=28, skill_tags=[MOTOR_THEFT], seniority=STANDARD, last_assignment_date=2024-03-08. Both handlers: active_claim_count < 40 (BR-001 ✓), peril match (BR-002 ✓), reserve < £100k (BR-003 N/A). MoSCoW discipline — the 40% MUST rule

PriorityDefinitionRule for application
MUST The product has zero usable value without this. A system that does not deliver this requirement cannot go live. Apply to ≤40% of requirements. If you are tagging more than 40% as MUST, you are not making real priority decisions. Challenge every MUST: "what happens if we launch without this?" If the answer is "it's inconvenient but the system works" — it is not a MUST.
SHOULD Important and expected, but not blocking delivery if a documented reason for deferral exists. The delivery of SHOULD requirements is the first thing cut when implementation hits blockers. They must be genuinely deliverable in v1 — if not, mark as COULD.
COULD Desirable, but will be dropped if there is any time or complexity pressure. Treat as a backlog item with a spec reference. Claude Code implements MUSTs first — COULD items are only implemented if MUSTs and SHOULDs are complete.
WON'T Explicitly excluded from this spec. Has been discussed and consciously deferred. List every WON'T explicitly with a forward pointer. Claude Code must know these are out of scope — a missing WON'T list leads to AI implementing adjacent capabilities that weren't asked for.
